The Story: From Prices to Beliefs — Identifying Monetary Shocks
Through the Lens of Expectation Formation
================================================================

Core Pitch (one sentence):
"The same monetary surprise has different macroeconomic effects
depending on why market participants think it happened."


1. THE PROBLEM
--------------

Traditional high-frequency identification (HFI) of monetary policy shocks
relies on aggregate financial market prices — interest rate futures, treasury
yields, stock returns — measured in narrow windows around FOMC announcements.
These prices are equilibrium outcomes of heterogeneous beliefs. They tell us
WHAT expectations changed, but not WHY they changed.

This matters for two reasons:

(a) The "Why" Problem — Perceived cause shapes transmission.
    A 25bp rate surprise driven by "the Fed sees persistent inflation" and
    a 25bp surprise driven by "the Fed is responding to a one-off supply shock"
    are the same in the price data, but they imply very different expected
    policy paths, and therefore very different real effects. The causal
    mechanism that market participants attribute to a policy action determines
    how they adjust portfolios, investment plans, and hiring decisions.
    Existing HFI measures cannot distinguish between these cases.

(b) The "Who" Problem — Aggregate prices mask belief heterogeneity.
    Two completely different belief distributions can produce the same
    equilibrium price change. A +5bp move in fed funds futures could reflect
    unanimous agreement, or deep disagreement (half expecting +15bp, half
    expecting -5bp). These two scenarios have different implications for
    subsequent asset price dynamics and real economic responses. Aggregate
    prices are an insufficient statistic for the underlying belief structure.


2. WHAT WE DO
--------------

We use LLM-based structured extraction from sell-side analyst reports
(Goldman Sachs, J.P. Morgan, Morgan Stanley, etc.) to observe what
aggregate prices cannot reveal:

  - Individual-level forecasts: specific predictions about fed funds rate,
    GDP, inflation, unemployment — with numerical values, time horizons,
    confidence levels, and explicit revisions from prior forecasts.

  - Causal reasoning: the mechanisms analysts cite to justify each forecast.
    Not just "we expect 3 cuts in 2025" but "we expect 3 cuts because
    labor market softening will push unemployment above 4.5%, reducing
    wage pressure and allowing the Fed to ease." Each causal argument is
    classified by economic channel (inflation, labor, fiscal, etc.),
    policy direction (tighten/ease), and strength.

  - Cross-analyst heterogeneity: different analysts looking at the same
    data and the same FOMC communication, arriving at different forecasts
    through different causal models. This disagreement is directly
    observable in our data.


3. WHY THIS IS BETTER IDENTIFICATION
--------------------------------------

Our approach improves monetary shock identification on two margins:

Margin 1: Content-based decomposition (the "Why").
    Existing decompositions (Acosta 2023, Jarocinski & Karadi 2020) rely on
    statistical methods — heteroskedasticity across regimes, sign restrictions
    — to infer what type of shock drove a given FOMC event. These methods
    recover shock categories (demand, supply, monetary, news) but cannot
    observe the actual reasoning process. We directly observe the causal
    mechanisms that analysts attribute to each forecast revision. This allows:

    - Finer-grained shock classification beyond the standard 4-type taxonomy
    - Content-based validation: we can verify whether a statistically
      identified "demand shock" is actually perceived as demand-driven
      by market participants
    - Testing whether perceived cause matters for transmission: do
      "inflation-driven" and "growth-driven" monetary surprises of the
      same size produce different macro responses?

Margin 2: Belief distribution (the "Who").
    We observe the cross-section of analyst beliefs, not just their average.
    This provides:

    - Disagreement measures: the dispersion of forecasts and the dispersion
      of causal reasoning across analysts for the same FOMC event
    - Consensus vs. contested channels: for a given event, is the perceived
      cause widely agreed upon, or do analysts disagree about the mechanism?
    - Predictive content of disagreement: does higher disagreement in
      causal reasoning predict larger subsequent forecast errors, higher
      volatility, or different impulse responses?


4. THE KEY EMPIRICAL TESTS
---------------------------

Test 1: Does "Why" matter for transmission?
    Take FOMC events with similar-sized monetary surprises (measured by
    traditional HFI). Split them by the dominant causal reasoning in analyst
    reports. Estimate impulse responses separately. If the responses differ,
    perceived cause matters — and traditional HFI is missing a relevant
    dimension.

Test 2: Content-based vs. statistical decomposition.
    Compare our content-based shock classification with Acosta's
    heteroskedasticity-based classification. When do they agree? When they
    disagree, which predicts macro outcomes better?

Test 3: Does disagreement predict transmission?
    For FOMC events where analysts agree on the cause, vs. events where
    they disagree, are the subsequent macro responses different? Theory
    suggests that consensus events should produce cleaner, more predictable
    impulse responses.

Test 4: Analyst reasoning as instrument.
    Use the cross-sectional variation in analyst causal reasoning as
    instruments for decomposing the aggregate monetary surprise into
    components — an alternative to Acosta's regime-based identification
    that does not require discrete changes in Fed communication policy.


5. CONTRIBUTION RELATIVE TO KEY LITERATURE
--------------------------------------------

vs. Traditional HFI (GSS 2005, Nakamura & Steinsson 2018):
    They measure what changed. We measure why it changed, and show that
    the "why" matters for the "what happens next."

vs. Acosta (2023):
    He uses newspaper text to statistically decompose monetary surprises
    into perceived demand/supply/monetary components. We use analyst reports
    to directly observe the causal reasoning, providing content-based (not
    just statistical) identification, finer granularity, and a heterogeneity
    dimension he cannot access.

vs. Jarocinski & Karadi (2020):
    They use sign restrictions on stock returns and interest rates to
    separate monetary and information shocks. We show that stock returns
    are a poor separator (as Acosta also argues) and provide a richer
    decomposition based on observed reasoning.

vs. Bauer & Swanson (2023):
    They argue information effects are an omitted variable bias. Our data
    can directly test this: if analyst reasoning around FOMC events
    references Fed-revealed information (not just pre-existing macro news),
    then information effects are real, not spurious.

vs. Coibion & Gorodnichenko (2012, 2015):
    They study expectation heterogeneity using surveys (SPF, Michigan).
    Surveys provide point forecasts but no reasoning. We provide both
    forecasts and the causal models behind them.


6. DATA AND METHOD
-------------------

Source: Sell-side research reports from major investment banks, covering
FOMC meetings and US macro outlook, 2024-2025 (expandable).

Extraction pipeline:
  Step 0: Report type classification (structured forecast, market
          commentary, survey report, strategy note)
  Step A: Forward-looking forecast extraction with temporal filtering
  Step B: Causal reasoning extraction with causality standards,
          locality constraints, and direction/strength calibration

Quality validation: Hand-labeled audit on 6 reports (157 causal arguments,
90 forecasts) with iterative prompt improvement targeting >85% forecast
accuracy and >70% causal reasoning accuracy.


7. WHAT WE NEED TO FIGURE OUT
-------------------------------

(a) Sample scope: How many FOMC cycles do we need? How many banks/analysts?
    Minimum viable: ~20 FOMC events × 3-5 banks = 60-100 reports.

(b) Temporal alignment: Can we align analyst reports to FOMC windows
    tightly enough to construct "high-frequency" belief revisions?
    Alternative: use report publication dates and construct wider windows.

(c) Aggregation: How do we go from individual analyst forecasts + reasoning
    to a usable shock measure? Options include:
    - Majority-vote on causal channel → content-based shock classification
    - Weighted average of forecast revisions → belief-adjusted surprise
    - Disagreement index → second-moment measure

(d) Identification formalization: What are the precise identifying
    assumptions? How do we write down a model that maps analyst beliefs
    to structural shocks?
